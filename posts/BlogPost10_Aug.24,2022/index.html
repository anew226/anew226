iPad Pros, Siri and NLP

How far has voice recognition software progressed in the year 2022?

Aug.24, 2022

It has been suggested that, at some point, technicians will be able to use speech-to-text software to fill out paperwork. It's a very intriguing idea. So, I had to find out for myself.

The world of NLP (Natural Language Processing) is an ever expanding, multidisciplinary field of study, and it's a working technology that's changing the way we interact with our computers. For me, it brings up images of Star Trek and the crew of the Enterprise-D seamlessly interacting with the ship's computer using their voice. The Enterprise crew made it look easy. However, computational linguistics is tricky — it is a technology that we have been collectively working on since the 1950s.

Data Scientists are still busy training AI models using Machine Learning on large sets of data. And, if that sounds like gibberish to you, or you're curious and want to learn more, check out the links at the bottom of this post. There is some great stuff there.

Things That Interfere with Voice Recognition

According to totalvoicetech.com, there are '5 Things That Can Interfere With Your Voice Recognition Software'.

  1. Voices in the Background
  2. Speedy Talking and Dialects
  3. Music or Loud Noises in the Background
  4. Speaker's Distance from the Microphone
  5. Similar-sounding Words

I would even add a one more thing to that list:

  1. Industry-Specific Jargon / Vocabulary

These are all things that will interfere with voice recognition software in our shop at AJWT. In Montréal, we work in a noisy shop with a lot of background noise. There's the sound of hand tools clanking, power tools whirring, test rigs humming, other people talking, the sound of safety slides inflating, and sometimes music. Also, there's the factor of dialects. Montréal is a beautifully multicultural city with many languages, cultures, and accents from all over the world.

Level of background noise: (Note: these are not official numbers - decibel readings were taken using an app on my phone)

  • My bench during lunchtime → 57 dB average
  • My bench during working hours → 64 dB average
  • Test rig while rig is on → 70 dB average
  • My bench while safety slide is inflating → 84 dB average

Although the background noise is greatest while inflating safety slides, this only happens for a moment and then it's over. Since the test rig is an area with prolonged, elevated background noise, I tested the voice recognition software while testing a unit. When I held the microphone closer to my mouth, I found the dictation was very accurate.

Adding Punctuation and Formatting Text

Let's look at Apple's Documentation: Dictate text on iPad.

While dictating text, say the punctuation or formatting.

For example, "Dear Mary comma the check is in the mail exclamation mark" becomes "Dear Mary, the check is in the mail!" Punctuation and formatting commands include the following:

  • period
  • comma
  • exclamation point
  • question mark
  • dollar sign
  • open parenthesis ... close parenthesis
  • quote ... end quote
  • new paragraph
  • new line
  • colon
  • semicolon
  • cap-to capitalize the next word
  • caps on ... caps off-to capitalize the first character of each word
  • all caps-to make the next word all uppercase
  • all caps on ... all caps off-to make the enclosed words all uppercase
  • no caps on ... no caps off-to make the enclosed words all lowercase
  • no space-to eliminate the space between two words (not available for all languages)
  • no space on ... no space off-to run a series of words together (not available for all languages)
  • smiley-to insert :-)
  • frowny-to insert :-(
  • winky-to insert ;-)
  • hash tag

Training Siri on My Voice

To train Siri to recognize your voice better, go into settings on your iPad and then slide the 'Listen for "Hey Siri"' toggle to the right.

Next, you will be prompted to 'Set Up "Hey Siri"'. Click Continue.

Now, this is your chance to train Siri on your voice. You will be prompted to read aloud five phrases into your iPad.

When you're done, you'll get a message to tell you that '"Hey Siri" Is Ready'.

Letting App Learn from Interactions

To help improve the accuracy of dictation, let's ensure that Siri is learning from the App that we're using.

Siri Learning from My Corrections

Now that I know Siri is learning from the App, I'll try some more dictation. This time I'll go back and manually correct the errors, then I'll repeat the phrase.

For the sake of brevity, I'll concentrate on only one phrase.

First dictation attempt:

  • Spring (PN: 340926) is warn.

Manually correction

  • Spring (PN: 340926) is worn.

Second dictation attempt:

  • Spring (PN: 340926) is warn.

Manually correction

  • Spring (PN: 340926) is worn.

Third dictation attempt:

  • Spring (PN: 340926) is worn.

Success! No more manual corrections required.

After only a few manual corrections, the accuracy of the dictation improved.

SiriKit

There's another tool that can be used to increase the accuracy of dictation, it involves registering custom vocabulary with SiriKit.

Apps adopt SiriKit by building an extension that communicates with Siri, even when your app isn't running. The extension registers with specific domains and intents that it can handle. Siri handles all of the interaction, including the voice and natural language recognition, and works with your extension to get information and handle requests.

This something that is worth looking into further for our own Quantum Web App.
