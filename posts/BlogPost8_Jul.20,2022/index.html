From email-0 to email-hero

Before you send a bad email that keeps you up at night and makes you wish you could just hit the 'unsend' button, let's learn some basic email etiquette. (It's not so easy to 'unsend' a bad email. Sometimes it's impossible.)

(If you really must unsend an email, try How to Unsend an Email in Gmail, Outlook, Yahoo. Best Ways Explained.)

Syntax.fm episode 477: Tips for Communicating

Syntax.fm is my favourite podcast to listen to on the long ride to work. Scott Tolinski and Wes Bos are two web developers who create a ton of great online content — everything from JavaScript, CSS, node modules, BBQ tips, git workflows, break-dancing, soft skills, and, of course, web development.

Wes is a web developer from Hamilton, Ontario, Canada — so, you should be huge fans of the show because Wes is Canadian — and Scott is a break-dancer and web developer from Denver, Colorado, USA. Together, they do an amazing job of teaching all things web to tens of thousands of dev fans. They're also a wonderful mood booster.

Here is a quick summary of this episode 477:

Tip #1: Number Your Asks

When you're asking someone questions in an email, it's a good idea to number your questions.

When Wes was emailing a store about buying a desk, he asked:

  1. How much is it?
  2. Is it still in stock?
  3. When can it be delivered?

Tip #2: Bold the Important Parts

If there is a question or a piece of important information you want the reader to see, bolding the most important part makes it easy for the recipient to pick out the important details.

Tip #3: Use Paragraphs

Avoid writing big, long tomes with no paragraphs. It makes it impossible to catch all the important details.

If you have a new idea, make a new paragraph.

Tip #4: Using '...'

Avoid using the '...' ellipsis. It's better to get your ideas together, type them up, then send. No one wants to read a long email chain of partial thoughts followed by '...'.

Tip #5: Error on the Side of Friendly

It's better if you can make your email sound like you're smiling and having a nice conversation.

  • Use only one '!' not '!!!'
  • Avoid Smilies in your emails :-D
  • Have a good tone: professional + a little bit friendly

Don't just use straight facts. It often comes off as terse - especially if the facts include bad news.

Tip #6: Recognize a Big Ask

Big asks are asking for something that the other person may or may not be able to do for you.

Example: When Wes emailed Lee from Vercel, he wrote, "Hey, I know this is a bit of a big ask. But do you think we could get back on our sponsored plan for the syntax website?" (Basically, can I have something for free?)

  • If it is a big request, give them an out: "If not, I understand. I am happy to pay for it as well."
  • (Don't use a nervous out: "or whatever! lol")

Tip #7: Mind the CC's

But first, before the tips, let's have a little CC and BCC primer:

What does CC mean?

  • CC stands for Carbon Copy
  • Recipients who are CC'd will see everyone else's email
  • 'Reply all' will send to all people in 'TO:' and 'CC' fields
  • Direct 'Reply' only goes to person who drafted message

It is a good idea to use CC to keep other people in the loop, to introduce someone, to represent a team, or to show someone that you do not need a response.

It's NOT a good idea to use CC to "copy up" (using CC to intentionally make someone look bad), to introduce someone without their consent, when you want a response from the recipient, when expecting an extended response from the main recipient, or when trying to inflate the size of your organization.

What does BCC mean?

  • BCC stands for Blind Carbon Copy
  • Recipient receives email as if they were CC'd, but recipient will not see other people on email chain
  • BCC recipients cannot see each other
  • BCC is like stealth mode

It's a good idea to use BCC to send mass messages to a large group of people, to maintain the privacy of recipients, or to spare someone from lengthy email threads.

It's NOT a good idea to use CC to "copy up" (to use BCC to intentionally make someone look bad), to introduce someone without their consent, to get a response from a recipient, to put people in the middle of a long email chain they have no interest in, or to exaggerate the size of your organization.

Now, back to the tips:

Do not CC everyone on everything!

Don't do a passive-aggressive CC. Don't email someone, "So, I just wanted to check in on that project you were supposed to have finished last week," and then CC that person's boss.

Don't break the email chain: Use 'Reply all' when replying to an email that has other people CC'd. Although the people who are on the CC list are not expected to reply, there's probably a good reason why they're being included in the loop.

Next Level: When someone CC's you in an email to introduce you to someone in another part of the company (or another company), thank the person, and then move them to BCC. Example: Scott introduces Wes to someone at Auth0. So, Wes sends a quick "thank you" email to Scott for the introduction, and then moves Scott to BCC (o Scott isn't included in the 'Reply all' emails between Wes and Auth0.

If you're confused about CC and BCC, check out these two very informative articles:

  • What Does CC Mean in Email, and How to Use it Properly
  • BCC For Email: What it Means and When to Use it
